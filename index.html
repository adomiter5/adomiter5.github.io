Introduction

This anthology of children’s poems highlights the various roles of animals in children’s literature. In this collection, animals are defined by the Oxford English Dictionary as “any such living organism other than a human being”. Moreover, for the purpose of this anthology “children’s poems” refers to poems written for children as well as those that were written with children in mind. The author argues that animals are salient and recurring characters in many forms of children’s literature. Therefore, this collection of poems will illustrate the role of animals in poetry and whether these roles change with respect to various constructs of childhood (e.g. Puritan, Enlightenment, Romantic). The anthology was created with the following questions in mind: What is significant about the animal(s) used in these poems? How are they different or similar to humans? What is gained or lost by using the animal(s)?

Overall, these poems reveal that throughout the course of time, animals take on many roles from teacher/mentor to companion to the entertainer. Poems written under the Puritan and Englightenment views of childhood tend to emphasize the qualities that separate humans from animals. Animals are thus used to educate children on what to do and what is right. In contrast, poems written under the Romantic view of childhood call attention to the connection between animals and the natural world. Interestingly, even though animals are seen as companions to humans, the role of the animal is still to teach. Lastly, in relation to nonsense literature, although the role of the animal is still to teach, they are given human-like qualities and have the additional role of entertainer. These roles are discussed in greater detail in the following sections. Despite a few differences, it is clear that animals themselves bring meanings to these poems that human characters alone can not do.
